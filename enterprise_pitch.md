## I. The Context

The companies that navigated the energy transition successfully were not the ones with the best technology arguments. They were the ones with the best intelligence on where sentiment was forming, where it was moveable, and where it was hardening into political opposition before that opposition could consolidate. The fracking wars produced a dominant American energy position, but only for the companies that could see the terrain clearly enough to act rather than react.

AI transition is running the same structural pattern at higher stakes and greater complexity. The current fights over data centers, energy consumption, privacy, and algorithmic accountability are the early terrain. They will resolve because the capital deployed is too large and the national interest is too clear for the transition not to happen on American terms. What comes after that resolution is the transition that matters: the economic and social reorganization that follows AI adoption at scale. That is where the policy fights will be fought, where public sentiment will harden or shift, and where the companies operating with better intelligence will separate from the ones reacting to headlines.

Even before the move to AI, companies' public affairs and external engagement were already costly and highly siloed. Different data sets, gaps in data-driven decisionmaking, and duplicative strategies already created friction and reduced efficiency. The organizations making the largest bets on the AI transition are operating with the same fragmented intelligence infrastructure that was already underperforming before the acceleration began.
