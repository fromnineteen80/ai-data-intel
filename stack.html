Data Acquisition
Cost to Us by Phase

The pre-money phase runs entirely on public data. The goal is to prove the methodology works before spending on licensed datasets. If the argument does not hold on free data, paid data will not fix it.

Congressional district and zip code geography makes every dataset in the stack legible to a policymaker, a district, and an electorate. Without it we have data. With it we have a decision tool. Pew microdata serves as a sentiment benchmark and calibration layer until the first proprietary polling wave runs in Phase 2. By the end of Phase 1 the platform has a working model, a technology stack built on Claude Code, and a proof of concept ready for a first client conversation. Cost: zero.

Phase 1 gives us structure and geography. Phase 2 adds the layer every competitor misses by not truly blending consumer and community and voter perspectives. Doing so creates a stack that cleans up what has always been messy in every organization and helps teams make more strategic and data driven decisions that can enrich actions going forward. It also cuts down on the need for siloed and redundant consulting costs in the era of agentic AI.

That is what makes our platform worth paying for.

Tunnl resolves public data into individual-level records. Revelio adds near real-time workforce movement by occupation and pace, drawn from public professional profiles. The LegiScan upgrade moves from weekly snapshots to real-time monitoring across federal and all 50 states, which is what the stakeholder management system needs to be operationally useful rather than a research reference.

The voter file ties polling respondents and audience segments to electoral history, modeled partisanship, and district-level turnout. We should confirm with Tunnl first, as their infrastructure likely includes voter file access already. The first polling wave is the most important addition in the stack. It is the first proprietary data in the lake, and the point at which the time-series starts compounding.

Phases 1 and 2 give us geography, legislative context, workforce movement, and first-party polling. What they do not give us is individual-level financial exposure. Lightcast and Experian ConsumerView close that gap. Instead of knowing that people in a district are anxious about AI, we can show which occupations within that district are most exposed, how their credit stress profiles compare, and how that has shifted over six months. That specificity is what makes the platform decision-grade rather than directional.

Lightcast covers workforce demand by occupation, geography, and income band. Paired with Revelio from Phase 2, we have both sides of workforce transition in the same stack. Experian ConsumerView adds income and credit stress at the cohort level, tying sentiment to real financial exposure. That linkage is the core claim of the platform.

The second polling wave begins the longitudinal panel, re-contacting prior respondents to build the time-series. Every cycle adds depth, raises switching costs, and makes the model more predictive. By the end of Phase 3 each client engagement generates signal that improves the platform for every other client.

Methodology Notes

On Tunnl: Modeled as $0–$100K. Free if Tunnl is a structural partner; $50K–$100K if brought in on a negotiated equity or revenue-share license. Treated as $0 in the low-end calculation throughout. This is the single biggest swing variable at Phase 2.

On voter file: L2 is the recommended vendor — bipartisan, widely used in academic and nonpartisan research contexts. National licensing runs $50K–$100K annually. If Tunnl comes in as a partner, their infrastructure almost certainly includes voter file access already, making this redundant. Confirm before contracting.

On Pew Research: Free, in Phase 1 as a sentiment benchmark and validation layer. Used to calibrate in-house polling waves and anchor the Institute’s public outputs. Not a replacement for proprietary polling.

On MCP, agent, and API: All three are built with Claude Code in Phase 1 at no external cost. Hosting and runtime costs begin in Phase 2 as they move to production. Anthropic API costs scale with agent query volume; Phase 3 figures are incremental over Phase 2, not duplicative.

On what is excluded: Nielsen, Comscore, Mastercard SpendingPulse, JPMorgan Chase Institute, Komodo Health, CoreLogic, Zillow, Equifax, TransUnion. All are redundant with public sources, misaligned with the thesis, or appropriate only as client-vertical upsells at a later stage.

On the Phase 3 ceiling: At $445K–$1.15M annually with Symphony Health included, four founding clients at $250K each covers the stack at the low end. Symphony is a vertical-specific add for health and pharma clients; without it the ceiling is $370K–$950K. Scope Symphony to population-level utilization and formulary context only. Full prescriber-level feeds are a separate negotiation and a materially larger cost.
